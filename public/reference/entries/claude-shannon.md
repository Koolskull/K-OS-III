# Claude Shannon

Claude Elwood Shannon (1916–2001) was an American mathematician and electrical engineer whose work founded the field of information theory and laid much of the conceptual groundwork for digital communication and computation as practiced today.

Shannon's 1937 master's thesis at MIT, *A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits*, applied Boolean algebra to the design of electrical switching circuits, demonstrating that the operations of formal logic could be implemented in physical hardware using relays. The thesis is sometimes described as the most important master's thesis of the 20th century; whether or not that ranking holds, the conceptual move it made — that logic and electronics could be unified — is a precondition for every digital computer that followed.

His 1948 paper *A Mathematical Theory of Communication*, published in two parts in the *Bell System Technical Journal*, defined information as a measurable quantity (the *bit*), introduced the concept of channel capacity, and proved fundamental theorems on what kinds of communication are possible over a noisy channel and at what rate. Information theory, as it now exists across telecommunications, data compression, error correction, cryptography, and (increasingly) machine learning, descends from this paper.

Shannon also did foundational work in cryptography during World War II at Bell Labs, much of which was classified at the time and later released as *Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems* (1949), establishing the mathematical basis for cipher analysis. He built early chess-playing programs, mechanical maze-solving mice, and an array of unusual machines (juggling robots, a flame-throwing trumpet) that reflect a recreational and exploratory cast of mind not always associated with foundational theorists.

A consistent feature of Shannon's published work is that the formal results are stated in language a careful non-specialist can follow. The 1948 paper is, despite its mathematical depth, unusually readable; readers who want to encounter the foundation of information theory directly are well-advised to read it themselves rather than rely on summaries.
