# Ada Lovelace

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace (1815–1852), was an English mathematician and writer best known for her work on Charles Babbage's proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine. Her published "Notes" on Luigi Menabrea's 1842 sketch of the Engine include what is widely considered the first algorithm intended to be processed by a machine — a method for computing the Bernoulli numbers — and an early articulation of the idea that a computing machine could process not just numbers but symbols of any kind, including music.

Her seven Notes (labeled A through G), published in 1843, run substantially longer than the article they annotate. Note G contains the Bernoulli-numbers algorithm. Note A makes the conceptual leap that the Analytical Engine, by virtue of operating on symbols according to rules, was not merely a calculator but something whose application could extend beyond mathematics — a claim that prefigures the modern conception of general-purpose computation by roughly a century.

There is ongoing scholarly discussion about how to attribute specific contributions in the Notes between Lovelace and Babbage, given their close working correspondence. The current consensus among historians of computing — represented in Hollings, Martin, and Rice's 2018 study — is that the Notes are substantively Lovelace's work, drawing on extensive prior correspondence with Babbage but reflecting her own analytical voice and her own framing of the Engine's significance.

Lovelace died of cancer at 36. Her work was largely forgotten until Bertram Bowden reprinted the Notes in his 1953 anthology *Faster Than Thought*, after which her place in the history of computing was steadily reassessed. The U.S. Department of Defense's programming language Ada, designed in the late 1970s, is named for her.
