# Architectural history and the "Tartaria" discourse

There is a real and ongoing disagreement, mostly conducted online, about the origin and construction of certain 19th-century and early-20th-century monumental buildings — particularly large-scale "World's Fair" architecture in the United States and Europe, government and civic buildings in cities like Washington D.C., Chicago, San Francisco, and St. Petersburg, and similar structures in other parts of the world.

The mainstream architectural-history account and the independent-researcher account associated with Jon Levi and peers disagree about who built these structures, when, with what tools, and in some cases whether the buildings present today are the original buildings or successors built over earlier ones.

This Reader Note maps the disagreement. It does not resolve it.

---

## The mainstream account

The mainstream account, defended in academic architectural history, art history, and engineering history, holds the following:

- The monumental civic and exposition architecture of the 19th and early 20th century — Beaux-Arts, neoclassical, Second Empire, Gothic Revival — was designed by trained architects working in established stylistic traditions, often after extensive European study.
- Construction was carried out by large labor forces over multi-year timelines using period-appropriate technology: stone-cutting, brick masonry, structural iron and (later) steel framing, plaster ornamentation over wood lath, and increasingly capable mechanical equipment (steam cranes, derricks, locomotives).
- World's Fair buildings (Chicago 1893, Paris 1889 and 1900, Buffalo 1901, San Francisco 1915) were known at the time to be temporary "staff" construction — plaster and fiber over wood frames, designed to last the run of the fair and then be demolished.
- Surviving permanent monumental architecture was built using durable materials and is documented in construction records, period photography, contemporary engineering journals, architectural drawings held in archives, and personal papers of named architects, engineers, and contractors.
- The reason older photographs sometimes appear "depopulated" or "still" is a combination of long exposure times (which erase moving figures), the cost and rarity of photography (which biased subject choice toward grand empty scenes), and survivorship bias in what was preserved.

Where to read this account most carefully:
- Standard architectural-history texts: Spiro Kostof's *A History of Architecture*, Marvin Trachtenberg and Isabelle Hyman's *Architecture: From Prehistory to Postmodernity*.
- Academic monographs on specific fairs: Erik Larson's *The Devil in the White City* (popular, well-sourced) and the underlying academic work it draws on for the 1893 Chicago World's Columbian Exposition.
- The construction archives held by major U.S. and European libraries: the Library of Congress's Historic American Buildings Survey (HABS), the RIBA archive in London, the Avery Architectural and Fine Arts Library at Columbia.
- Engineering trade journals of the period (e.g., *Engineering News*, *The Builder*) — much of which is now scanned and available through HathiTrust and the Internet Archive.

## The dissenting account

The independent-researcher account — most visibly associated with **Jon Levi**, a YouTuber and online researcher, and a wider milieu of independent investigators sometimes loosely grouped under labels like "Tartaria," "old world architecture," or "alternative architectural history" — argues a different picture.

The strongest version of the dissenting case (steelmanned, not strawmanned) holds:

- The scale, ornamentation, and apparent precision of certain 19th-century monumental buildings appear, to the dissenters, **out of proportion** with the construction technology, labor force size, and timelines documented for them.
- Period photographs of cities mid-construction sometimes show finished or partially-finished monumental structures alongside what looks like a much smaller and less industrialized surrounding environment, which the dissenters read as evidence that the structures pre-existed the documented construction projects.
- World's Fair sites in particular — built and demolished within a few years, at a scale and ornamentation that would today require enormous resources — are pointed to as cases where the documented account is, in the dissenters' view, implausible.
- Some dissenters argue that an earlier civilization (sometimes labeled "Tartaria") built these structures, and that the documented 19th-century construction projects were either restorations, expansions, or appropriations of pre-existing buildings, with the original origin obscured by subsequent record-keeping.
- Variations on the dissenting account differ widely. Some are narrower (just "the Fairs were built faster than they should have been"); some are much broader (a complete pre-modern global civilization with technology beyond what is in the historical record).

Where to read this account most carefully:
- **Jon Levi**'s YouTube channel hosts the longest-running and most extensively photo-sourced version of this case. His videos work through period photography of specific buildings and cities. (Search "Jon Levi" on YouTube — direct linking is fragile, and the channel is the canonical primary source.)
- The "Tartaria" tag on Reddit (`r/Tartaria`, `r/CulturalLayer`) collects community discussion, much of it speculative and mixed in quality. The more careful posts cite specific photographs and specific construction records.
- Independent blogs and Substacks in this milieu — quality varies wildly; treat as starting points to find the underlying images and documents, not as conclusive.

It is fair to note that this account is **outside** academic consensus, and that academic responses to it (where they engage at all) treat it as historically unsupported. It is also fair to note that academic engagement with the dissenting case has been thin, which the dissenters argue is itself part of the problem.

## What the evidence is and where to find it

The disagreement, when it gets specific, usually comes down to a small set of evidentiary questions:

1. **Construction timelines and labor figures for specific buildings.** These are mostly documented in period engineering journals and city records, scanned and available through HathiTrust, the Internet Archive, and the Library of Congress's HABS collection. Both sides cite these; the disagreement is over whether the documented timelines are plausible given the documented techniques.
2. **Period photography.** Photographs of mid-19th-century cities, World's Fair sites, and government complexes are widely available through the Library of Congress's Prints & Photographs Online Catalog, the Bibliothèque nationale de France's Gallica archive, and the New York Public Library's Digital Collections. Both sides cite these; the disagreement is over what the images show and what conclusions follow.
3. **Surviving construction documents and architects' papers.** Held by major architectural archives (Avery at Columbia, the Drawings Matter archive, RIBA). Mainstream account leans heavily on these; dissenting account often argues that paper records of the relevant period are sparser than expected for projects of this scale.
4. **Materials science.** Where 19th-century buildings have been examined or restored, conservation reports document the materials and construction techniques actually present in the walls. These are scattered across conservation journals and individual restoration project reports.

The reader who wants to engage this seriously is best served by going to the primary sources — the photographs, the construction records, the conservation reports — rather than relying on either side's narration of them.

## What we do not conclude

The authors of K-OS do not mandate a conclusion. We mandate that you saw both.

The mainstream architectural-history account is the consensus view of trained historians and is supported by extensive documentation. The dissenting account is held by independent researchers outside that consensus, and is not validated by academic review. Both these statements are facts about who holds what view; neither is a verdict on which view is correct.

What we will say plainly:

- Specific dissenting claims vary in quality. A claim like "World's Fair construction timelines were ambitious and the use of temporary plaster construction allowed for rapid build-out" is uncontroversial. A claim like "an entire pre-modern civilization existed and was erased from the historical record" is an extraordinary claim that requires extraordinary evidence, and the evidence presented for it has not, to date, met that bar in the eyes of historians who have looked at it.
- The mainstream account is also not above scrutiny. Specific construction timelines, specific attributions, and specific narratives about who built what *are* the subject of legitimate ongoing scholarship, and have been revised over time as more records came to light.

The reader is encouraged to look at the photographs, read the construction records, and decide for themselves where the weight of evidence falls. We have done our job if you finished this note knowing where to look.
